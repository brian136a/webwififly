## What the User Sees

A clean, minimalist landing page with:

- **Top-left:** Small cyan WiFi icon (subtle branding)
- **Center:** Large, bold headline: *"Welcome to Wififly."*
- **Below headline:** Secondary copy: *"Find out if you are getting the speeds you are paying for."*
- **CTA:** Single cyan button labeled *"Test"*
- **Background:** Dark gradient (blue to black)
- **Visual style:** Calm, professional, minimal noise

## Critical UX Observations

### Strength #1: Restraint & Respect
The page demonstrates confidence in the value proposition by **not overselling**. It trusts the user understands why checking WiFi speed matters.

**Why this is Kiwi-appropriate:**
- Confident but unpretentious
- Assumes intelligence without being condescending
- Lets the value speak for itself

**Competitive advantage:** Most speed test apps are covered in claims, graphics, and pressure. This page is refreshingly calm.

### Strength #2: Zero Cognitive Load
One button. One promise. No decisions to make. The user knows exactly what action to take next.

**Psychology principle:** When decision complexity is low, click-through rates are high. This page nails it.

### Strength #3: Visual Calm
Dark theme with accent cyan color creates a modern, trustworthy feel without aggression.

## Friction Point #1: No Expectation-Setting

### The Problem
Users don't know what they're about to enter:
- "Will this take 5 minutes or 30 seconds?"
- "Is this a free test or will I be asked to sign up?"
- "What happens at the end ‚Äî do I get results immediately?"
- "Is this going to try to sell me something?"

### User Anxiety
*"If I click this, what am I committing to?"*

This hesitation could cost you 20-30% of potential users before they even enter the app.

### The Fix
Add micro-copy **below the CTA button:**

```
‚úì Free test ‚Ä¢ ‚úì No signup required ‚Ä¢ ‚úì Results in 3 minutes
```

**Why this works:**
- Removes three major barriers in three words
- Emphasizes "free" early (huge conversion lever)
- Sets clear time expectation
- Uses checkmarks (visual trust signal)

## Friction Point #2: Vague Value Proposition

### The Problem
"Find out if you are getting the speeds you are paying for" assumes:
1. The user knows their plan speed (many don't)
2. The user understands why it matters (speed varies by room, time of day, etc.)
3. The user feels frustration about this (they might think their WiFi is "fine")

### User Mental Model
**For some users:** "Yes, I wonder if I'm being ripped off"  
**For others:** "My WiFi is okay... I don't think this is for me"  
**For privacy-conscious:** "Wait, they want to know my plan speed? Why?"

### The Fix
**Option A (Better for conversion):**
Replace headline with a question that's more emotionally resonant:

```
"Is Your WiFi Letting You Down?"
```

OR

```
"Why Is Your WiFi So Slow?"
```

**Why:** Positions the app as a solution to felt pain, not an abstract test.

**Option B (Alternative):**
Keep headline, but add emotional subtext:

```
"Welcome to Wififly.
Find out why your WiFi isn't doing what it should.
(Spoiler: You might be paying for speeds you're not getting.)"
```

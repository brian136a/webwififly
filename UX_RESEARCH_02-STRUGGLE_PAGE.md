## Friction Point #3: Assumes Problem Acknowledgment

### The Problem
The headline "Tired of Bad WiFi? You're Not Alone" assumes **active frustration**.

Some users might not think their WiFi is "bad":
- "My WiFi is okay for what I use it for"
- "It's fine most of the time"
- "I just want to know if I'm getting what I pay for"

### User Mental Model (Non-Frustrated User)
*"I mean, I'm not tired of my WiFi... I just want to check. Is this page for me?"*

### The Risk
A user who thought their WiFi was adequate might feel invalidated or confused by the frustration-focused messaging.

### The Fix

**Option A (Slightly soften headline):**
```
FROM: "Tired of Bad WiFi? You're Not Alone."
TO: "How's Your WiFi Really Performing?"
```
More inclusive; doesn't assume frustration; applies to all users.

**Option B (Make it collaborative):**
```
"Let's Check Your WiFi Together"
```
Positions the app as a companion, not a problem-solver.

## Friction Point #4: The Gap Between Emotion and Action

### The Problem
The struggle cards create strong emotional validation, but don't explain **what happens next**:
- "Will I learn WHY my WiFi is slow?"
- "Will you give me solutions or just data?"
- "Is this free or am I about to be upsold?"
- "How long will this take?"

### User Uncertainty
*"I've recognized my problem. Now what? I'm going to click this button, but I don't really know what I'm getting into."*

This is actually fine for *entering* the funnel, but creates hesitation for some users.

### The Fix (Optional)
Add small confidence-builder **below the green CTA:**

```
"See exactly how your WiFi performs in every room. 
Get honest insights. No jargon."
```

**Why this helps:**
- Reiterates the benefit (room-by-room breakdown)
- Reinforces tone ("honest," "no jargon")
- Addresses the "what happens next" question subtly
